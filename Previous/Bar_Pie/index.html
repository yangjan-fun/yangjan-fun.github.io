By 2019, the world's population had reached 7.7 billion. Globally, about 26% of the world's population is under the age of 15 and about 9% is over 65. In Europe, the gap is much smaller, with 16% of the population under 15 and 18% over 65. With the exception of a few countries, such as the Middle East and sub-Saharan Africa, population growth rates have fallen sharply in many regions. Fertility rates may be high in these high-growth countries, where women have an average of five or more children. However, about 50% of the world's population lives in low-fertility countries, where women have fewer than 2.1 children. In Europe, where most of the population used to be working-age adults with few dependents, the trend is expected to reverse soon, with many developed countries expected to have more elderly people than the young by 2050.

Six of the seven continents on earth are home to large-scale permanent settlements. Asia is the most populous continent (includes China and India, the world's two most populous countries), with a population of 4.601 million, accounting for 60% of the world's population. Africa is the second most populous continent in the world, with a population of 1.308 billion, accounting for 17% of the world's population. Europe's 747 million people make up 10 percent of the world's population. South America, with an estimated population of 427 million, accounts for 6%. North America consists mainly of the United States and Canada, with a population of 367 million (5%), while Oceania, the least populous, has 42 million residents (0.6%). (Please note: numbers and percentages are estimated.)
